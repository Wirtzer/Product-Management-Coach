# Jobs to Be Done (JTBD)

**Purpose:** Understand what customers are really trying to accomplish — the "job" they "hire" a product to do — rather than focusing on features or demographics.

**Best for:** Customer research, product positioning, competitive analysis, identifying unmet needs.

**Origin:** Developed by Clayton Christensen (Harvard Business School) and further refined by Tony Ulwick (Outcome-Driven Innovation) and Bob Moesta (demand-side innovation).

---

## The Core Concept

People don't buy products. They **hire** products to do a job.

A customer doesn't buy a drill because they want a drill. They hire a drill to make a hole. And they don't really want a hole — they want to hang a shelf. And they don't really want a shelf — they want a tidy, organized home.

Understanding the real job changes everything:
- **Competition:** A milkshake's real competitor might be a banana, a bagel, or boredom — not just other milkshakes.
- **Innovation:** Once you know the job, you can often find a better solution than the existing category.
- **Positioning:** "We help you [do the job]" beats "We have [feature list]."

---

## Job Statement Format

A well-formed job statement follows this structure:

```
When [situation], I want to [motivation], so I can [expected outcome].
```

**Examples:**
- "When I'm commuting alone, I want something to make the drive less boring, so I can arrive at work in a good mood."
- "When I'm planning a team project, I want to see everyone's availability, so I can find a meeting time without 12 emails."
- "When I'm learning a new technology, I want to quickly understand the key concepts, so I can have informed conversations with my engineering team."

**Rules for good job statements:**
- Situation-specific (not universal)
- Solution-agnostic (no product names or features)
- Stable over time (jobs don't change as fast as technology)
- Observable (you can see someone struggling with this job)

## The Three Layers of Jobs

### Functional Job
The practical task the customer is trying to complete.
*"I need to get from home to work."*

### Emotional Job
How the customer wants to feel (or avoid feeling) during and after.
*"I want to feel like my time isn't being wasted."*

### Social Job
How the customer wants to be perceived by others.
*"I want to look like someone who makes smart choices."*

**Products that nail all three layers create intense loyalty.** A Tesla satisfies the functional job (transportation), the emotional job (excitement, control), and the social job (innovation, environmental consciousness).

## Outcome-Driven Innovation (Ulwick's Extension)

Tony Ulwick extended JTBD into a quantitative method:

### Step 1: Define the Core Job
What is the customer fundamentally trying to accomplish?

### Step 2: Map the Job Process
Break the job into steps (typically 8-15):
1. Define what they need
2. Locate inputs
3. Prepare to do the job
4. Confirm readiness
5. Execute the job
6. Monitor progress
7. Modify as needed
8. Conclude the job

### Step 3: Identify Desired Outcomes
For each step, what outcomes does the customer want?
Format: **Minimize [undesired outcome]** or **Maximize [desired outcome]**

Example (job: plan a meal):
- Minimize the time it takes to decide what to cook
- Minimize the likelihood of choosing something the family won't eat
- Minimize the number of ingredients I need to buy

### Step 4: Score Importance vs. Satisfaction
Survey customers:
- "How important is this outcome?" (1-10)
- "How satisfied are you with current solutions?" (1-10)

**Opportunity Score = Importance + (Importance - Satisfaction)**

High importance + low satisfaction = **underserved need** = opportunity.

## The "Switch" Interview (Moesta's Method)

Bob Moesta developed a specific interview technique to uncover jobs:

### The Four Forces of Progress
When someone switches from one product to another, four forces are at play:

```
         PUSH (current frustration)
              ↓
[Old Product] ─────────────────→ [New Product]
              ↑                       ↑
         PULL (attraction)      ANXIETY (new risk)
                                HABIT (inertia)
```

1. **Push:** What's frustrating about the current solution?
2. **Pull:** What's attractive about the new solution?
3. **Anxiety:** What worries them about switching?
4. **Habit:** What keeps them using the old solution?

**To drive adoption:** Increase push and pull. Decrease anxiety and habit.

### Interview Timeline
Ask customers to reconstruct their decision timeline:
- First thought: When did you first realize you needed something different?
- Passive looking: When did you start noticing alternatives?
- Active looking: When did you start comparing options?
- Decision: What tipped the decision?
- Purchase: What was the buying experience?
- First use: What happened when you first used it?

## How to Apply in PM Work

### Product Discovery
- Interview 10-15 customers about recent decisions
- Map the jobs they're hiring your product (and competitors) to do
- Identify underserved jobs that no product handles well

### Feature Prioritization
- Score features by which job they serve and how underserved that job is
- Features serving well-served jobs are "nice to have"
- Features serving underserved jobs are high-priority

### Competitive Analysis
- Map competitors by which jobs they serve, not by feature comparison
- Identify jobs where no current solution does well → whitespace opportunity

### Positioning
- Position your product around the job, not the feature
- "We help you [job]" is more compelling than "We have [feature]"

---

## How to Use in Interviews

**When asked "How would you identify customer needs?":**
1. "I'd use a Jobs to Be Done approach — understanding what customers are trying to accomplish, not just what they say they want."
2. Describe the Switch Interview method
3. Give an example of how a job reveals unexpected competitors

**When asked "How would you improve [product]?":**
1. Identify the core job the product serves
2. Map the job into steps
3. Identify where users struggle (high importance, low satisfaction)
4. Design solutions for those gaps

**Key insight:** "JTBD reveals that your real competitors aren't always who you think. A project management tool isn't just competing with other PM tools — it's competing with spreadsheets, email chains, and sticky notes. Understanding the job tells you who you're really up against."
