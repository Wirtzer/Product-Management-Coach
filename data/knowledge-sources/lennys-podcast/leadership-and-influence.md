## Key Frameworks

### Sales Before Logistics — Wes Kao, Ep #45
People chronically overestimate how much buy-in their audience already has. They jump into logistics (the how, the process) before securing agreement on the what and why. A head of operations struggled to get executives to fill in weekly wins. She led with mechanics (which document, what format) and got crickets. When she reframed it as celebrating and motivating teams, people engaged.

**How to apply:** Even 30 seconds of framing — "Here's why we're doing this, what problem we're solving" — dramatically increases the likelihood of action. Always sell before you logistic.

### MOO: Most Obvious Objection — Wes Kao, Ep #45
Before presenting anything, ask: what are the obvious objections someone might raise? You don't need to anticipate every possible pushback, but you can absolutely anticipate the obvious ones. If you voice the objection yourself and address it upfront, you preempt defensiveness and demonstrate thorough thinking.

**How to apply:** Put "MOO" on a Post-it somewhere visible. Before every email, proposal, or meeting, spend 2 minutes identifying the 1-2 most likely pushbacks and address them proactively.

### CEDAF: Delegating Without Losing Standards — Wes Kao, Ep #45
A framework for maintaining quality while actually letting go:
- **C: Comprehension** — Does the person have everything they need? Access, context, definition of done.
- **E: Excitement** — Are you framing it as important? Connect to company priorities or career growth.
- **D: De-risk** — What could go wrong? Start small if risk is high.
- **A: Align** — Give space for questions. They might reveal gaps in your own thinking.
- **F: Feedback** — Shorten the loop. Check in after a day, not a week.

**How to apply:** Run through this mentally before every delegation. Also works for prompting AI — Claude and ChatGPT produce better work with comprehension, context, reasoning, and explicit feedback.

### The Coaching vs. Telling Spectrum — Rachel Lockett, Ep #100
When a direct report brings you a problem: do they need direction (tell) or do they need to build their own problem-solving capacity (coach)? Most leaders default to telling because it feels faster. But coaching builds the team's independence, which scales. The question "What options have you considered?" transforms the conversation.

**How to apply:** Default to coaching first. Only tell when: (1) there's genuine urgency, (2) the person lacks the foundational knowledge, or (3) you've coached multiple times on the same issue with no improvement.

### Difficult Conversations as a Skill — Dr. Becky Kennedy, Ep #115
Child psychology principles apply directly to adult workplace dynamics. The core insight: most interpersonal friction comes from people whose emotions have overwhelmed their capacity to communicate. The intervention: separate the person from the behavior, validate the emotion, then address the issue.

**How to apply:** In feedback conversations, lead with "I know you care about [X], and that's why I want to talk about [specific behavior]." This frames the conversation as aligned, not adversarial.

### Low Heart Rate Presence — Sam Lessin, Ep #111
Etiquette isn't social theater — it's a practical skill that builds trust and reduces friction. The framework: show up with composure and genuine confidence by (1) knowing the context, (2) being present rather than performing, (3) making others comfortable. People who can maintain a "low heart rate" in high-stakes rooms earn disproportionate trust.

**How to apply:** Before important meetings, spend 5 minutes preparing not just your content but your presence. What does the room need from you? Confidence? Calm? Energy? Adjust accordingly.

## Mental Models

### Strategy, Not Self-Expression — Wes Kao, Ep #45
The goal of feedback is behavior change. Everything else — venting, establishing fairness, making sure they know they messed up — is self-expression that often backfires. Trim 90% of what you initially want to say and keep only what motivates change.

### Conciseness Is Clarity, Not Brevity — Wes Kao, Ep #45
Being concise is about economy of words, not word count. A 300-word email can be tighter than a 1,000-word memo. The bottleneck to conciseness isn't discipline — it's unclear thinking. Even 60 seconds of preparation before a meeting eliminates 20-30% of unnecessary content.

### The Blast Radius of Poor Communication — Wes Kao, Ep #45
A confusing Slack message in a 15-person channel generates multiplied friction — everyone asking clarifying questions, everyone interpreting differently. Three extra minutes of clarity saves hours across the organization. This is high-leverage work.

### Confidence Calibration — Wes Kao, Ep #45
Accuracy in language is credibility. If you have a hypothesis, say "This might X" — not "This will X" unless you're certain. But equally: don't undermine a strong, data-backed recommendation with false humility. Match your conviction level to your evidence level.

### Give Away Your Legos — Molly Graham, Ep #109
In high-growth companies, your job changes every 6-12 months. The leaders who scale are those who proactively hand off responsibilities they've outgrown and take on bigger, messier problems. Holding on is the opposite of leadership.

## Top Lessons

1. **Communication is the job itself** — Wes Kao, Ep #45
   If you're not getting the outcomes you want, the problem is likely how you're explaining yourself. "If I'm not getting the reaction I'm looking for, how might I be contributing to that?" This reframe means you have more control than you think.

2. **Feedback should be rare and focused** — Rachel Lockett, Ep #100
   Don't save up a list of grievances. Make one clear point about the behavior and its impact, then listen. The research-backed approach changes behavior; the dump-everything approach creates defensiveness.

3. **Signposting reduces cognitive load** — Wes Kao, Ep #45
   Use structural language: "For example," "Because," "The most important part is…" This guides readers through complex information without relying on heavy formatting. Especially powerful in long strategy documents.

4. **Vent before you feedback** — Wes Kao, Ep #45
   Vent to a friend, therapist, or partner first so you arrive at the conversation grounded and clear. Then focus exclusively on what behavior needs to change and why it matters.

5. **Both Wes Kao (Ep #45) and Rachel Lockett (Ep #100) converge on this:** the highest-leverage leadership skill is knowing when you're about to communicate reactively and choosing instead to prepare. Even 60 seconds of preparation transforms outcomes.

6. **Preparation reveals your real opinion** — Wes Kao, Ep #45
   Rambling in meetings happens because you're processing in real time. The solution isn't "be more concise" — it's "know what you think before you speak." Preparation is the only reliable fix.

7. **The Switch Log for self-awareness** — Rahul Vohra, Ep #42
   Log every task switch via Slack DM. At week's end, graph the data. Vohra discovered he spent only 6-7% of time on his zone of genius despite thinking otherwise. Track actual work, not intended work.
